OpenStreetMap Data Report

This year for State of the Map we look at the last six years of OpenStreetMap's growth and change.

Founded in 2005, OpenStreetMap has grown from a local project to a worldwide map with widespread use and high data quality.

Users

On January 6, 2013, OpenStreetMap crossed the 1 million users mark, and continued to grow, both in terms of data and active userbase.

Like most internet projects, a majority of users are casual or inactive, with a small minority contributing the majority of additions and corrections to the map.

Data

OpenStreetMap's database, called the 'Planet', has grown tremendously over time, as well as other forms of user-contributed data, like GPX tracks from GPS devices.

Regions

Many variables affect how active OpenStreetMap users are in each region. Some areas are quickly, exhaustively mapped after natural disasters, while others have committed groups of local enthusiasts.

The early OpenStreetMap project was focused on the United Kingdom, with most of the rest of the data in a few places in Europe. The map is comparatively tiny in North America, except for some areas of detail in Wisconsin and around the Chesapeake Bay. The relative prominence of Antarctica shows how little map data there was for most parts of the world.

Although active mapping continued in Europe, and other areas of the world began to be filled in, the OpenStreetMap globe was massively reshaped in late 2007 and early 2008 by the months-long import of the U.S. Census Bureau's TIGER/Line map data for the United States.

With the United States map filled in, the bulk of mapping activity moved back to Europe, where by mid-2009, the work put into mapping Germany made it visually dominate the cartogram.

2010 and 2011 saw great increases in mapping in Canada, France, and Japan, as well as continued additions and corrections in large US cities.

The most recent available OSM history file, from February, 2013, shows some recent mapping in New Zealand, and that the map in Canada and Russia has continued to improve. It also highlights, though, how disproportionately well mapped Europe and the United States continue to be compared to the rest of the world, in spite of some recent work in Africa and South America.

Highlights

The explosive growth of OpenStreetMap has required multiple large reinventions: rewriting and redacting history, introducing new, better APIs, and more.

In Chicago, Ian Dees led an import of building data from the city government that both improved addressing to locations in the city and made the map more useful.

The bot-mode automated editor swept of the US in late 2012 and early 2013 to expand imported, shortened road names into their full versions.
